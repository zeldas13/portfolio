Color Theory refers to the study of how colors work together to influence perceptions and evoke emotions.

Definitions

  1. Primary colors ‐ pure; cannot be created by mixing other colors. The three primary colors are red, blue, and yellow.
  2. Secondary colors ‐ formed by mixing two primary colors. The three secondary colors are green, violet, and orange.
  3. Tertiary colors ‐ formed by mixing a primary color with a secondary color. Some examples of tertiary colors include blue-violet, yellow-green, and red-orange.
  4. Triadic colors ‐ colors that are spaced out in even increments on the color wheel, such as red, yellow, and blue.
  5. Complementary colors ‐ colors that are opposites on the color wheel, such as blue and orange.
  6. Split colors ‐ a base color and two colors that would be balanced on the opposite side of the color wheel. An example would be a base color of red — the split-complements would be blue-green and yellow-green. 1
  7. Analogous colors ‐ colors that share a space on the color wheel, such as orange, red-orange and red.
  8. Saturation ‐ the degree of purity, brightness, and vividness within a color.
  9. Hue ‐ the purest form of a color.
  10. Tint ‐ created when white is mixed into a color.
  11. Shade ‐ created when black is mixed into a color.
  12. Value ‐ the relative lightness or darkness of a color.
  13. Contrast ‐ difference in hue.
  14. Vibrancy ‐ refers to the intensity of a color. A high-vibrancy color is saturated and bold, while a low-vibrancy color is more muted.
  15. Monochromatic ‐ colors very blended with minimal hue variety.
  16. Color scheme ‐ combination of all colors used within a project, arranged in a manner that enhances the overall design.
  17. Neutral colors ‐ often perceived as the absence of color, they actually typically contain subtle undertones. Some examples of neutral colors include gray and brown.
  18. Warm colors ‐ bring energy and intensity to a design, and can often invoke strong emotions. Some examples of warm colors include red and orange.
  19. Cool colors ‐ contribute to a calm and soothing atmosphere, and can invoke feelings of peace and tranquility. Some examples of cool colors include blue and purple.
  20. Whitespace ‐ empty space.
